*Part 1 of a series on composite roles in the AI era*

For decades, specialization has shaped organizational structure. Roles were confined to single disciplines, grouped into functions, and staffed through skill taxonomies prioritizing depth over breadth. This model functioned well when cross-discipline coordination was expensive and change occurred slowly.

However, actual work has always exceeded formal job descriptions. Deloitte's research indicates that "71% of surveyed workers already perform work outside the scope of their job descriptions" while only 24% report performing identical work to colleagues with matching titles and levels. AI isn't creating this gap—it's accelerating its visibility.

## The Composite Role Concept

A **composite role** deliberately integrates capabilities from multiple traditionally separate disciplines into a single performer, designed to reduce coordination costs in environments where AI has made single-discipline work insufficient.

Bell Labs pioneered this approach with the "Member of Technical Staff" designation, emphasizing flat structure and cross-disciplinary contribution. Modern companies including OpenAI, NVIDIA, and Snowflake have revived this model.

Dr. Sarabeth Berk Bickerton's research on hybrid professional identity shows that individuals increasingly fuse multiple professional identities (researcher, designer, strategist, educator) into unified professional selves, yet traditional titles fail capturing this integration.

## The Forward Deployed Engineer: Case Study

Forward deployed engineers—positions integrating software engineering, product thinking, and client consulting—represent this shift. Job postings increased over 800% between January and September 2025. Analysis of 1,000 FDE postings revealed that 79% are vertical-agnostic, requiring engineers who learn new domains rapidly rather than specialize narrowly.

## Three Key Takeaways

### 1. Composite Roles Address a Fundamental Coordination Problem

Hackman and Oldham's Job Characteristics Model identifies five dimensions predicting motivation and performance: skill variety, task identity, task significance, autonomy, and feedback. Forward deployed engineers score high across all dimensions.

Thompson's organizational theory demonstrates that when technical change accelerates and single-discipline work becomes insufficient, rational organizational design collapses coordination costs through integrated roles.

The Tavistock Institute's sociotechnical systems research established that changing technical systems requires simultaneous social system redesign, or performance degrades. Composite roles represent exactly this joint redesign.

### 2. Expertise Doesn't Require Narrow Specialization

While Ericsson's deliberate practice framework emphasizes domain-specific expertise, Epstein demonstrates that impactful performers in complex environments develop breadth alongside depth.

AI tooling enables this integration by lowering the skill floor in adjacent disciplines. Strong engineers can now leverage AI for accelerated domain learning, client documentation, and prototyping across unfamiliar technical stacks—collapsing what previously required specialist teams into single capable performers augmented by AI.

Berk Bickerton's framework identifies the "hybrid zone" as the distinctive value intersection where fused identities develop. Without organizational recognition, individuals performing this integration remain invisible to hiring, promotion, and development systems.

The Harvard Business Impact 2025 study found that static, role-based workforce planning fails because AI transforms roles nonlinearly. IDC's 2024 EMEA Employee Experience Survey found that 63% of European workers anticipate job automation within two years, with 70% of new positions directly influenced by AI technology.

### 3. Deliberate Design vs. Accidental Emergence

Morgeson and Humphrey's Work Design Questionnaire demonstrates that job design predicts motivation, role clarity, and retention.

Karasek's demand-control model predicts that high-demand, low-control work creates strain. Undesigned composite roles are inherently high-demand and low-control, lacking formal authority across integrated disciplines.

Even well-designed composite roles face risks. PwC's 2025 AI Jobs Barometer found that AI-exposed roles concentrate disproportionately in white-collar occupations, intensifying strain on exactly those most likely in composite roles and risking burnout regardless of role design quality.

## Methodology for Effective Design

Cognitive task analysis, developed by Crandall, Klein, and Hoffman, extracts cognitive demands, decision requirements, and judgment patterns from expert performance. Applied to composite roles, CTA reveals where integration occurs—which decisions require simultaneous multiple disciplinary perspectives and where judgment cannot decompose into separate functional inputs.

Enid Mumford's ETHICS methodology from Manchester Business School emphasized participative design giving workers direct input into role structure around new technology. This principle directly applies to composite role design: performers themselves offer the best insights into where integration happens, cognitive load created, and where systems break down.

## Conclusion

Forward deployed engineers validate what observers have long recognized: "the future of AI-era work is not narrower specialization but deliberate integration across disciplines." This integration collapses distance between building and deployment, between technical capability and organizational context. It's not optional—it's the mechanism enabling successful AI implementation in complex environments.

The concerning pattern is that most organizations don't deliberately design these roles; they allow emergence, then wonder why performers burn out. The EU AI Act, effective February 2025, now requires AI system providers and users maintaining staff with adequate AI literacy, increasingly demanding composite capabilities bridging technical knowledge with domain expertise, compliance, and operational realities.

Organizations applying engineering rigor to role design will build demanded AI-era workforces. Others will continue writing job descriptions listing twelve competencies and hope circumstances prove favorable.
